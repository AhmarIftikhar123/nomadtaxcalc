## The Situation Most Spain Nomads Are Actually In

Let's use a real scenario.

**Meet Marco.** He's a German freelance developer. He spent 2025 like this:

- 🇪🇸 **Spain (Barcelona):** 195 days
- 🇩🇪 **Germany (Berlin):** 95 days
- 🇬🇧 **England (London clients):** 70 days working remotely

His annual income: **€60,000** — all earned remotely from German and British clients.

Marco's assumption: *"I earn from Germany and England, not from Spain. Spain can't touch my income."*

**Marco is wrong.** Here's why.

---

## Why Spain Can Tax Your Foreign Income

Once you spend **more than 183 days in Spain in a calendar year**, Spain considers you a **tax resident**. And Spanish tax residents are taxed on their **worldwide income** — not just money earned from Spanish clients.

That means Marco's German and British client invoices are in scope for the Spanish tax authority (AEAT).

> **One more thing most people miss:** Spain counts **both the day you arrive and the day you leave** as Spanish days. So 183 calendar days booked can easily become 185 counted days.

Marco at 195 days: **Spanish tax resident**. Worldwide income applies.

---

## So Does Marco Pay Tax in Three Countries?

Not exactly — and this is where tax treaties come in.

Spain has **Double Taxation Agreements (DTAs)** with both Germany and the UK. These treaties are legally binding rules about which country gets to tax which income. You are **not** taxed twice on the same money.

Here's how it works for Marco:

**Spain (primary country):**
Marco is a Spanish tax resident, so Spain goes first. It applies the standard **IRPF progressive rates** (19%–47%) to his worldwide income of €60,000.

At €60,000, Marco's Spanish IRPF is approximately **€16,800–€18,000**, depending on his regional deductions and filing status.

**Germany:**
Under the **Spain-Germany DTA**, Germany applies the *exemption-with-progression* method. Germany does not tax the income that Spain has already taxed — but it does use Marco's total income to determine the rate applied to any remaining German obligations. His German Finanzamt liability: effectively reduced to near zero for this income.

**England (UK clients):**
Marco's UK clients pay him, but Marco is not a UK tax resident (only 70 days there, well below the UK's 183-day threshold, and he has no UK ties triggering automatic residency). The UK does not tax him. His UK clients pay him gross — no PAYE, no withholding.

**Marco's result:**
- Owes: Spain (primary)
- Germany: minimal or zero after treaty credit
- UK: zero
- **Total effective rate: ~28–30%** on €60,000

## What About the Beckham Law?

You may have heard that Spain offers a special flat 24% tax rate for digital nomads — this is called the **Beckham Law** (Régimen Especial de Trabajadores Impatriados).

Here's the key thing to know:

| | Beckham Law | Standard IRPF |
|---|---|---|
| Rate | 24% flat | 19%–47% progressive |
| Taxes | Spanish income only | Worldwide income |
| Who qualifies | Employees of foreign companies | Everyone (default) |
| Freelancers | ❌ Usually no | ✅ Yes |
| Deadline | Apply within 6 months of DNV | n/a |

**For Marco:** As a self-employed freelancer (autónomo), the Beckham Law generally **does not apply**. He falls under standard IRPF.

## The 183-Day Rule — Counting It Correctly

A few things that catch nomads off guard:

- **Arrival day counts.** So does departure day. Both are counted as Spain days.
- **Short trips abroad** (a weekend in Morocco, a week in Portugal) — Spain may count these back in as Spanish days when assessing "habitual residence."
- **The count resets on January 1.** It's per calendar year, not a rolling 12 months. Arrive July 1 and stay until December 31 = 184 days = Spanish tax resident for the entire year.
- **Under 183 days is not always safe.** If your spouse and children live in Spain, or your main business is registered there, Spain can still claim your tax residency via the *"center of vital interests"* rule.

Our tool shows a **near-threshold warning** when you enter a day count within 30 days of triggering residency in any country — so you can see the risk before it becomes a problem.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need to pay tax in both Spain and the UK?**
Not on the same income. The Spain-UK Double Taxation Agreement ensures Spain goes first (if you're resident there) and the UK gives you a Foreign Tax Credit. You'll need to notify HMRC by filing form P85 to stop being a UK taxpayer.

**What if I'm a US citizen living in Spain?**
US citizens are taxed on worldwide income regardless of where they live — so you file with both Spain (as a resident) and the IRS. The Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE — ~$126,500 for 2025) can offset your US liability if you qualify via the Physical Presence Test. Our tool calculates FEIE eligibility automatically when you select US citizenship.

**Does the Digital Nomad Visa give me a tax benefit?**
No — the DNV is a visa status, not a tax benefit. The Beckham Law flat rate is a separate application you file with AEAT within 6 months of your DNV approval. Miss the window and you're on standard IRPF.

**What if I spend less than 183 days in Spain?**
You are a Spanish non-resident. Spain can only tax income you earn *from Spanish sources*. Your home country likely remains your primary tax country — but check: the UK doesn't automatically stop taxing you just because you left.

**Can I model the Beckham Law in your tool?**
